An Advanced AI Practitioner's Course

A six-week, one-hour-per-day curriculum.
From fluent user to architectural thinker.

You already talk to AI the way most people talk to colleagues — with context, nuance, and expectation. You've built websites, run complex creative collaborations, designed content strategies, wrestled with philosophical questions about machine consciousness, and used AI as genuine intellectual infrastructure. You are not a beginner. Most courses would bore you within ten minutes.

This course starts where you actually are and pushes into territory that separates people who use AI from people who think in AI — who understand the architecture beneath the conversation, who can build systems rather than fire off prompts, and who can see both the extraordinary capability and the structural limitations clearly enough to work at the genuine frontier.

The title borrows from your own instinct: scan the treeline. Know what's coming. Build before you need to.

Course Philosophy

Pedagogy Spiral curriculum — each week revisits earlier concepts at greater depth. Theory is never separated from practice. Every session ends with something you've made.
Assessment No tests. You build a portfolio of artefacts — tools, workflows, documents, web applications — that prove competence by existing.
Domains Every exercise is rooted in your actual life. Clinical medicine, literary fiction, ecological land management, web design, strategic resilience planning.
Principle Nothing generic. Nothing hypothetical. Nothing you couldn't use the same afternoon you learn it.

Week One

The Engine Room

Understanding what's actually happening beneath the conversation

Day 1 The Context Window as Architecture
+
Concept

The context window isn't a chat history — it's a stage. Everything the model sees exists simultaneously. There is no real "memory" — only what's loaded into the window right now. Understanding this changes how you structure every interaction.

Explore
  • Open a new Claude conversation. Ask: "What is the first thing I said to you?" Notice that it knows. Now understand why it knows — not because it remembers, but because the full transcript is re-presented every turn.
  • Ask Claude to estimate how much of its context window your conversation currently occupies. Discuss what happens as you approach the limit.
Build

Write a system-prompt-style preamble (300–500 words) that would set up Claude as a collaborator on Dog Years. Include: your writing voice, the collection's thematic arc, the stories that exist, the ones in development, your editorial preferences. Think of this as furnishing the stage before the actor walks on.

Field Notes

How does thinking of context as architecture (not conversation) change what you'd front-load into a session?

Day 2 Tokenisation and the Shape of Language
+
Concept

AI doesn't read words — it reads tokens. English prose tokenises efficiently; code, poetry, and non-English text often don't. The "length" of your context window depends partly on what kind of language fills it.

Explore
  • Tokenise: a paragraph of Dog Years prose, the same content as bullet points, a block of HTML/CSS from your website, a medical case summary with abbreviations. Compare token counts.
Build

Take one of your Dog Years story pages (the HTML file). Estimate its token count. Now write a compressed reference version — same essential information in 40% fewer tokens. This is the art of context-window efficiency: saying more with less.

Field Notes

Where in your current AI use are you "spending" tokens wastefully? Where could compression help?

Day 3 System Prompts — The Invisible Hand
+
Concept

Every conversation with Claude begins with a system prompt you never see. It shapes tone, capability, safety behaviour, and persona. Understanding system prompts means understanding why Claude behaves the way it does.

Explore
  • Try giving Claude an explicit persona: "You are a gruff Scottish veterinarian with 40 years of experience." Then ask it a medical question. Notice how persona shapes not just tone but what information it foregrounds.
Build

Write three different system-prompt-style openings for the same task (editing a Dog Years story). One positions Claude as a literary editor, one as a sensitivity reader, one as a structural analyst. Run the same passage through each. Compare and document the differences.

Field Notes

What did the persona experiment reveal about how framing shapes output?

Day 4 Failure Modes and Hallucination Anatomy
+
Concept

AI fails in predictable, structural ways. Understanding why it hallucinates (pattern-completion without grounding), when it's most likely to confabulate (obscure facts, precise numbers, recent events), and how to detect it makes you dramatically better at this.

Explore
  • Deliberately try to make Claude hallucinate. Ask about: a very specific but obscure clinical trial in addiction medicine, the publication history of a fictional author you invent, detailed statistics about Scottish SDCF outcomes.
  • When it produces confident-sounding answers, fact-check them. Map the failure pattern.
Build

Create a personal "hallucination field guide" — a short document listing the types of queries where you've found AI most and least reliable, with examples from your actual domains.

Field Notes

How does understanding hallucination change your trust calibration?

Day 5 Temperature, Sampling, and the Randomness Dial
+
Concept

AI responses aren't deterministic — they're probabilistic. "Temperature" controls how much randomness enters the output. Low temperature = predictable, convergent. High temperature = surprising, divergent. This matters enormously for creative vs. analytical work.

Explore

Ask Claude to write the same scene from Dog Years three times: once "as safely and predictably as possible," once "with moderate creative risk," and once "with maximum creative divergence." Notice the differences in word choice, structure, and surprise.

Build

Map your regular AI tasks onto a temperature spectrum. Clinical information → low. Brainstorming → high. Editing prose → medium. Website code → low. Create a visual reference chart.

Field Notes

Week 1 synthesis. What has shifted in your understanding? What questions have opened up?

Week Two

Prompt Architecture

From individual prompts to systems of prompts

Day 6 The Anatomy of an Expert Prompt
+
Concept

Great prompts have structure: role (who is the AI being?), context (what does it need to know?), task (what should it do?), constraints (what should it avoid?), format (how should the output look?), and examples (what does good look like?). Not every prompt needs all six, but knowing the toolkit matters.

Explore

Take five of your recent Claude conversations. Reverse-engineer the prompts. Which elements were present? Which were missing? Correlate with output quality.

Build

Design a reusable prompt template for one of your recurring tasks — "Draft a Bluesky post for Dog Years" or "Review this passage for pacing and voice." Make it modular: a skeleton you can fill in quickly each time.

Field Notes

Where does explicit structure improve your results? Where does it constrain them?

Day 7 Chain-of-Thought and Reasoning Scaffolds
+
Concept

Asking AI to "think step by step" genuinely improves reasoning by forcing intermediate tokens that inform subsequent output. But you can go further: you can design the reasoning scaffold. Your PGCE brain will recognise this immediately.

Explore

Take a complex clinical scenario — polysubstance use, unstable housing, new medical complication. Ask Claude to reason through it three ways: directly, with chain-of-thought, and with a structured scaffold you provide. Compare depth and quality.

Build

Create a clinical reasoning scaffold — a reusable framework for presenting complex cases to AI. Test it against three different scenarios. Refine it.

Field Notes

How does scaffold design compare to lesson planning? What makes a good scaffold?

Day 8 Structured Outputs — XML, JSON, Markdown as Control
+
Concept

Asking AI to produce structured output — XML tags, JSON objects, Markdown with specific headings — gives you programmable results. This is the bridge between conversational AI and AI-as-infrastructure.

Explore

Ask Claude to analyse a Dog Years story and return its assessment as free prose, then as a structured Markdown template, then as XML tags. Notice how format shapes content, not just appearance.

Build

Design a structured output template for your Bluesky content calendar: post text, image suggestion, hashtags, posting time, engagement hook, and a "why this post matters" note. Generate a week's posts in one go.

Field Notes

Where in your workflow would structured outputs save you time or improve consistency?

Day 9 Few-Shot Learning — Teaching by Example
+
Concept

The most powerful way to control AI output is to show it what you want. Few-shot prompting — providing 2–5 examples of input→output pairs — lets the model learn your style and quality bar in-context, without any training.

Explore

Take three of your best Bluesky posts. Feed them as examples, then ask for five new ones in the same voice. Compare with posts generated without examples. Do the same with Dog Years prose.

Build

Create a "voice library" — a curated set of 3–5 exemplar passages from your own writing that you can drop into any prompt to calibrate output. Organise by type: narrative prose, social media, clinical writing, reflective essay.

Field Notes

How does few-shot compare to verbal instruction? When is showing better than telling?

Day 10 Adversarial Prompting and Red-Teaming Your Work
+
Concept

One of the most advanced uses of AI is turning it against your own output — using it as a deliberate devil's advocate, stress-tester, hostile reader. Not about being negative; about building robustness.

Explore
  • Take a piece of writing you're satisfied with. Ask Claude to find the three weakest sentences, argue the opposite thesis, read it as someone who dislikes the genre, and identify where a hostile reviewer would attack.
Build

Design a "red team" prompt for Dog Years editorial work — a reusable framework that stress-tests a story before you consider it finished: structural critique, voice consistency, anachronism scan, emotional pacing, and a "would this survive a workshop?" assessment.

Field Notes

Week 2 synthesis. How has your relationship with prompting changed?

Week Three

Beyond the Chat Window

The API, automation, and programmable intelligence

Day 11 API Fundamentals — Your First Programmatic Call
+
Concept

The chat window is a consumer product. The API is the raw material. It gives you control over system prompts, temperature, token limits, model selection, and — critically — the ability to chain calls together programmatically.

Build

Set up an Anthropic API key. Make your first API call using curl or Python. Send a simple message and receive a response. If Python is unfamiliar, use Claude itself to help you write the script — that's not cheating, it's meta-learning.

Field Notes

What feels different about API access vs. the chat window? What possibilities do you immediately see?

Day 12 System Prompts and Model Parameters in Practice
+
Concept

Via the API, you can set system prompts, adjust temperature, choose models, set max token limits, and control stop sequences. Each parameter is a lever. Learning to pull them deliberately is what separates a user from a practitioner.

Build

Create a script with configurable parameters. Use it to process a Dog Years passage through three different editorial lenses — structural editor, line editor, copy editor — saving each output. You've just built your first automated editorial pipeline.

Field Notes

Which parameters matter most for which tasks? Start building intuition.

Day 13 Prompt Chaining — Multi-Step Workflows
+
Concept

The real power of the API is chaining: output from one call becomes input to the next. This lets you build workflows that no single conversation could achieve — decomposing complex tasks into specialised steps.

Build

Implement a three-step chain in Python: (1) Haiku extracts themes and settings from a story, (2) Sonnet generates a Bluesky post from those themes, (3) Sonnet critiques the post for voice and accuracy. Each step calls the API, processes the output, and feeds the next.

Field Notes

What tasks in your life would benefit from decomposition into chains? List five.

Day 14 Batch Processing and Scale
+
Concept

Once you can make one API call, you can make a hundred. Batch processing lets you apply AI to collections of inputs — every story in Dog Years, every post in a calendar, every case in a dataset.

Build

Create a batch script that runs multiple story summaries through the same analytical prompt: word count, dominant themes, temporal setting, narrative voice, emotional register, dog's role. Output as a structured dataset (CSV or JSON).

Field Notes

Where else would batch processing be valuable? Clinical audit data? Garden planting records?

Day 15 Error Handling, Rate Limits, and Robustness
+
Concept

Real-world API use means dealing with failures: rate limits, timeouts, malformed responses. Building robust systems means anticipating failure and designing for graceful recovery.

Build

Add error handling to your batch script: retry logic, exponential backoff, logging, progress indicators. Make it production-quality rather than prototype-quality.

Field Notes

Week 3 synthesis. You now have programmatic access to AI. What does that change about how you think about your work?

Week Four

Tool Use and the Agentic Frontier

Making AI do things in the world, not just talk about them

Day 16 Tool Use — The Concept
+
Concept

Modern AI can use tools: web search, code execution, file manipulation, API calls. This transforms AI from a language machine into an agent — something that can perceive, reason, and act.

Explore

In Claude, deliberately trigger different tool uses. Notice how it decides which tool to use. Try to find the limits: what tools does it have that you hadn't realised? What tools does it lack?

Build

Map your ideal tool ecosystem — a diagram showing every tool you'd want an AI assistant to access in your daily work: clinical databases, story files, Bluesky, your website, calendar, garden planning data.

Field Notes

What are the ethical implications of agentic AI in your clinical work vs. your creative work?

Day 17 MCP — The Model Context Protocol
+
Concept

MCP is an open standard for connecting AI to external tools and data sources. Think of it as USB for AI — a universal connector. Understanding MCP means understanding how the next generation of AI integrations will work.

Build

Design (on paper) an MCP server for Dog Years. What tools would it expose? list_stories, get_story_metadata, search_themes, get_timeline, suggest_gaps. What resources would it offer? You don't need to build it yet — the design is the learning.

Field Notes

How does MCP change your mental model of what AI can do?

Day 18 Computer Use and Code Execution
+
Concept

Claude can write and execute code, create files, and interact with a computing environment. This means you can automate tasks that would normally require programming — data processing, file conversion, report generation.

Build

Use Claude to build a small tool: a script that takes a Dog Years story as text and generates a structured metadata card — title, word count, era, setting, characters, themes, estimated reading time. Output as both JSON and pretty-printed Markdown.

Field Notes

What tasks in your daily life involve repetitive data processing that could be automated?

Day 19 Web Research as a Compound Skill
+
Concept

AI-assisted research isn't just "search for X." It's compound: formulating queries, evaluating sources, synthesising, identifying gaps. Doing this well requires understanding how AI's search tool works and what it's good and bad at.

Build

Design a research protocol — a reusable prompt structure for deep research tasks. Include: query formulation, source evaluation criteria, synthesis format, confidence rating, and a "gaps and limitations" section. Test it on a real question from your clinical or creative work.

Field Notes

How does AI-assisted research compare to your own practice? Where does it excel? Where do you still need to do the work yourself?

Day 20 Building Your First Integrated Workflow
+
Concept

Everything converges. An integrated workflow chains API calls, tool use, structured outputs, and prompt architecture into a single system that accomplishes something meaningful.

Build

Choose one and build it:

  • Dog Years Editorial Pipeline: Upload draft → AI analyses structure, pacing, voice, anachronisms → generates editorial notes → suggests Bluesky posts for publication
  • Clinical Case Summariser: Input scenario → structured reasoning → evidence gap search → formatted case summary
  • South Acre Seasonal Planner: Current date + weather → cross-reference planting calendar, microseasons → prioritised task list
Field Notes

Week 4 synthesis. You've moved from talking to AI to building with it. What surprised you?

Week Five

Knowledge Systems and Critical Practice

Building personal knowledge infrastructure and developing a critical eye

Day 21 RAG — Retrieval-Augmented Generation
+
Concept

RAG gives AI access to your own documents at query time. Instead of relying on training data, you feed it your information and it reasons over that. The difference between an AI that knows the world and one that knows your world.

Build

Create a Claude Project for Dog Years editorial work. Upload: story files, collection overview, style guide, thematic timeline. Test with editorial questions. Document what it gets right and wrong.

Field Notes

Where would RAG most transform your work? What documents would you most want an AI to access?

Day 22 Evaluation — How Do You Know If It's Good?
+
Concept

Most people evaluate AI output by vibes. That works for casual use but fails at scale. Systematic evaluation means defining quality criteria, creating rubrics, and applying them consistently. Your teaching background is a superpower here.

Build

Design an evaluation rubric for your most common AI use case. 4–5 criteria, each scored 1–5 with anchor descriptions. Test it. Refine it. This becomes a permanent tool.

Field Notes

How does formalising evaluation change your relationship with AI output?

Day 23 AI Clichés, Sycophancy, and Voice Contamination
+
Concept

AI has characteristic failure modes in creative work: overuse of certain words ("delve," "tapestry," "nuanced"), sycophantic agreement, and a tendency to flatten distinctive voices into a generic AI register. Recognising and counteracting these patterns is an advanced skill.

Build

Create an "anti-AI-ism" style guide — words, phrases, and patterns you want Claude to avoid when writing in your voice. Include positive instructions too: "Use Anglo-Saxon monosyllables where possible," "Prefer concrete nouns." Add it to your Dog Years Project and test the difference.

Field Notes

What's the relationship between AI clichés and the loss of individual voice?

Day 24 Multi-Modal Thinking
+
Concept

AI can process images, PDFs, and structured documents. Understanding multi-modal input means you can use AI for tasks most people don't attempt: analysing photographs, interpreting charts, extracting data from scans, combining visual and textual reasoning.

Build

Create a multi-modal workflow: photograph from South Acre → species identification → habitat assessment → management recommendations → microseasons journal entry. Or: clinical PDF → extraction → comparison with current practice → gap analysis.

Field Notes

Where does multi-modal AI surprise you? Where does it still fall short?

Day 25 The Ethics and Politics of AI Use
+
Concept

You think about this already — but this session asks you to systematise it. Labour displacement, intellectual property, environmental cost, access equity, dependency risk, and the philosophical status of the systems themselves.

Build

Write a personal AI ethics statement — 500–1000 words — articulating your position on responsible AI use in your creative, clinical, and personal life. Not performative; a working document that guides practice. Let it be honest about tensions and unresolved questions.

Field Notes

Week 5 synthesis. How has your critical framework evolved? What practices will you change?

Week Six

Integration, Mastery, and the Frontier

Building capstone projects and looking ahead

Day 26 The Personal AI Toolkit
+
Concept

By now you have: prompt templates, evaluation rubrics, voice libraries, workflow scripts, research protocols, and an ethics statement. This session organises them into a coherent, maintainable personal toolkit — not a junk drawer, but a system.

Build

Create a structured repository containing all your artefacts, organised by domain. Write a brief README for your future self explaining each component and when to use it.

Day 27 AI-Powered Web Application
+
Concept

You build websites. You now understand the API. This session combines both: a web page that calls Claude and does something intelligent. This is about understanding that AI can be embedded in the things you build.

Build

Create an HTML page with JavaScript that calls the Claude API. A Dog Years Story Explorer, a South Acre Field Guide, or a Microseason Checker. Something real, something you'd actually use or share.

Day 28 Multi-Model Strategy
+
Concept

Different models excel at different things. Opus is deep and nuanced but slow. Sonnet is fast and capable. Haiku is lightning-quick and cheap. An advanced practitioner uses each strategically — sometimes chaining them.

Build

Design and document your personal multi-model strategy: which model for which tasks, with rationale. Test it by running the same editorial passage through Haiku (technical scan), Sonnet (structural analysis), and Opus (deep reading).

Day 29 The Capstone — Design
+
Concept

The capstone integrates everything into a single, substantial artefact that solves a real problem. Choose one:

  • The Dog Years Companion System: Claude Project with full context, prompt library, automated Bluesky pipeline, story analysis dashboard, web exploration tool.
  • The Clinical Intelligence Framework: Reasoning scaffold, research protocol, case summarisation pipeline, guidelines comparison tool, ethics framework.
  • The South Acre Observatory: Seasonal task planner, ecological monitoring framework, plant identification workflow, resilience planning tool, journaling system.
Build

Design the capstone on paper. Define: scope, components, tools required, data needed, success criteria, and a build plan for the final days.

Day 30 The Capstone — Build
+
Build

Execute your design. Use everything: API access, prompt architecture, structured outputs, tool use, evaluation rubrics, multi-model strategy. Document as you go.

Day 31 The Frontier
+
Concept

The course is over, but the frontier isn't. Where is the field going? Longer context windows, persistent memory, autonomous agents, multi-modal generation. What are the risks on the horizon? Deepfakes, labour displacement, dependency, the alignment problem at scale.

Build

Write a letter to yourself in six months. What should future-you know about the state of AI? What should you be watching? Building? Wary of? Post it somewhere you'll find it in September.

Field Notes

Final synthesis. How has this course changed your practice, your thinking, your relationship with AI? What's the most important thing you learned that you didn't expect?
